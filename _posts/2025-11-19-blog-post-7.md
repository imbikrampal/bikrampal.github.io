We consider two-dimensional turbulence on a periodic square domain $\Omega = [0,L]\times[0,L]$. The grid contains $N$ points in each direction, giving a uniform spacing $\Delta x = L/N$. The physical gridpoint coordinates are
$X_{ij} = iL/N,\; Y_{ij} = jL/N$ for $i,j = 0,\ldots,N-1$. All physical-space fields (vorticity, streamfunction, velocity, etc.) are defined on this grid. The solver stores the constants appearing in the 2D vorticity equation:

$$
\partial_t \omega + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega = \nu\nabla^2\omega - \mu\omega + f_\omega,
$$

where $\nu$ is the viscosity, $\mu$ is the Ekman drag, and $\Delta t$ is the timestep for temporal integration. The forcing is random and acts only on Fourier modes whose magnitude lies in a prescribed band $k_{\min}\le |\mathbf{k}| \le k_{\max}$. The forcing amplitude is normalized so that the real-space RMS forcing attains a desired value.

All parameters are stored for constructing the forcing operator. Due to periodicity, admissible Fourier modes are indexed by $n_x,n_y\in\{-N/2+1,\ldots,N/2\}$. The corresponding wavenumbers are
$$
k_x = \frac{2\pi}{L}\,n_x,\qquad k_y = \frac{2\pi}{L}\,n_y,
$$
with magnitude
$$
|k| = \sqrt{k_x^2 + k_y^2}.
$$

The streamfunction in Fourier space is obtained from vorticity via
$$
\psi(\mathbf{k}) = -\frac{\omega(\mathbf{k})}{k^2}.
$$
Since $k^2=0$ at the zero mode, the solver uses a safe definition
$$
k^2_{\mathrm{safe}} =
\begin{cases}
k^2, & (n_x,n_y)\neq(0,0),\\[4pt]
1, & (n_x,n_y)=(0,0),
\end{cases}
$$
so divisions never involve literal zeros. The solver analytically enforces $\psi(0,0)=0$. Nonlinear terms such as $\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega$ generate unresolved modes that would alias back into the resolved spectrum.

The spectral $2/3$ rule applies the Galerkin projection
$$
|n_x| \le \frac{2}{3}\,\frac{N}{2},\qquad |n_y| \le \frac{2}{3}\,\frac{N}{2},
$$
and sets all other modes to zero following each nonlinear product. This ensures mathematical consistency of the spectral method. The solver initializes the vorticity field in Fourier space with $\omega(\mathbf{k},0)=0$, which serves as the initial dynamical state to be advanced in time. The final call `init_forcing()` constructs the random forcing process in Fourier space.

## Computation of $\hat{\psi}$, $\hat{u}$ and $\hat{v}$

The code implements the relation
$$
\psi(\mathbf{k}) = -\frac{\omega(\mathbf{k})}{k^2},\qquad \psi(0)=0.
$$
Velocity is recovered from the streamfunction via $u=\partial_y\psi$, $v=-\partial_x\psi$. Fourier differentiation gives
$$
\widehat{\partial_y\psi} = i k_y \psi(\mathbf{k}),\qquad \widehat{\partial_x\psi} = i k_x \psi(\mathbf{k}),
$$
so that the spectral velocity components are
$$
u(\mathbf{k}) = i k_y \psi(\mathbf{k}),\qquad v(\mathbf{k}) = -i k_x \psi(\mathbf{k}).
$$
These expressions guarantee divergence-free velocity, since $k_x u(\mathbf{k}) + k_y v(\mathbf{k}) = 0$, hence $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}=0$.

## Scaled Forcing

The forcing is constructed in vorticity space as a frozen random spectrum supported on a prescribed wavenumber band. Define the forcing set
$$
\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{k} : k_{\min}\le |\mathbf{k}|\le k_{\max}\}.
$$
The solver implements this via a Boolean mask selecting only these modes. Each mode in $\mathcal{B}$ receives a complex unit-magnitude phase:
$$
\hat{g}(\mathbf{k}) = e^{i\theta(\mathbf{k})},\qquad \theta(\mathbf{k})\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,2\pi),
$$
yielding an isotropic, phase-randomized forcing spectrum.

Real physical-space forcing requires Hermitian symmetry, i.e.
$$
\hat{f}_\omega(-\mathbf{k}) = \overline{\hat{f}_\omega(\mathbf{k})}.
$$
The solver enforces this via reflection and averaging:
$$
\hat{f}_\omega(\mathbf{k}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\Big(\hat{g}(\mathbf{k}) + \overline{\hat{g}(-\mathbf{k})}\Big),
$$
ensuring that the inverse FFT yields a real field. The zero mode is suppressed,
$$
\hat{f}_\omega(0,0) = 0,
$$
since forcing the mean vorticity would imply an infinite circulation input. The forcing is finally projected onto the $2/3$–dealiased region,
$$
\hat{f}_{\omega,\mathrm{final}}(\mathbf{k}) = \hat{f}_\omega(\mathbf{k})\,\chi_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k}),
$$
eliminating all non-dealiased modes. Thus the frozen forcing spectrum stored by the solver is
$$
\hat{f}_{\omega,\mathrm{frozen}}(\mathbf{k}) =
\begin{cases}
e^{i\theta(\mathbf{k})}, & \mathbf{k}\in\mathcal{B}\ \text{and dealiased},\\[4pt]
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

Beginning with $f_\omega(\mathbf{k}) = \hat{f}_{\omega,\mathrm{frozen}}(\mathbf{k})$, the corresponding forcing streamfunction is
$$
\psi_f(\mathbf{k}) = -\frac{f_\omega(\mathbf{k})}{k^2}.
$$
The physical-space forcing velocities follow from the streamfunction relations $f_x = \partial_y\psi_f$, $f_y = -\partial_x\psi_f$, which in Fourier space yield
$$
\widehat{f_x} = i k_y \psi_f(\mathbf{k}),\qquad \widehat{f_y} = -i k_x \psi_f(\mathbf{k}).
$$
Inverse transforms give the real-space fields. The RMS magnitude of the forcing velocity is
$$
\mathrm{RMS}
= \sqrt{\frac{1}{L^2}\int_\Omega (f_x^2 + f_y^2)\,dA}
\approx
\sqrt{\frac{1}{N^2}\sum_{i,j}(f_x^2 + f_y^2)}.
$$

If this RMS value is nonzero, a scaling factor
$$
\lambda = \frac{A_{\mathrm{target}}}{\mathrm{RMS}}
$$
is applied so that the resulting forcing satisfies
$$
\sqrt{\langle (\lambda f_x)^2 + (\lambda f_y)^2\rangle} = A_{\mathrm{target}},
$$
where $A_{\mathrm{target}}$ is the forcing amplitude chosen by the user. Thus the final scaled vorticity-forcing spectrum returned each timestep is
$$
\hat{f}_\omega^{\mathrm{scaled}}(\mathbf{k}) = \lambda\,\hat{f}_{\omega,\mathrm{frozen}}(\mathbf{k}).
$$
The forcing is constructed as an isotropic, random, band-limited spectrum following the method of Eswaran and Pope.

## Nonlinear Term

The nonlinear term in the 2D vorticity equation is
$$
\mathcal{N}(\omega) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega = u\,\partial_x\omega + v\,\partial_y\omega.
$$
The program computes this term in physical space and returns its Fourier transform with proper de-aliasing. It starts by recovering the streamfunction and velocity:
$$
\psi(\mathbf{k}) = -\frac{\omega(\mathbf{k})}{k^2},\quad
u(\mathbf{k}) = i k_y \psi(\mathbf{k}),\quad
v(\mathbf{k}) = -i k_x \psi(\mathbf{k}).
$$
Using inverse FFTs,
$$
u(x,y) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[u(\mathbf{k})],\quad v(x,y) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[v(\mathbf{k})],\quad \omega(x,y) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[\omega(\mathbf{k})].
$$
Spectral differentiation gives
$$
\partial_x\omega(\mathbf{k}) = i k_x \omega(\mathbf{k}),\qquad \partial_y\omega(\mathbf{k}) = i k_y \omega(\mathbf{k}),
$$
and after inverse FFT,
$$
\omega_x = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[i k_x \omega(\mathbf{k})],\qquad \omega_y = \mathcal{F}^{-1}[i k_y \omega(\mathbf{k})].
$$
In physical space,
$$
\mathcal{N}(x,y) = u\,\omega_x + v\,\omega_y.
$$
In Fourier space $\widehat{\mathcal{N}}(\mathbf{k}) = \mathcal{F}[\mathcal{N}(x,y)]$. Using the $2/3$-rule mask $D(\mathbf{k})$,
$$
\widehat{\mathcal{N}}_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k}) = D(\mathbf{k})\,\widehat{\mathcal{N}}(\mathbf{k}).
$$
The Fourier-space vorticity equation is
$$
\partial_t \omega(\mathbf{k}) = -\widehat{\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega} - \nu k^2\omega - \mu\omega + \hat{f},
$$
so the program returns $-\widehat{\mathcal{N}}_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k})$ as the nonlinear contribution to the RHS.

## RK2 time stepping

We solve the Fourier-space PDE
$$
\partial_t \omega(\mathbf{k},t) = -\nu k^2\omega - \mu\omega + N(\mathbf{k},t) + f(\mathbf{k},t),
$$
where $N(\mathbf{k}) = -\widehat{\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega}$. Define the linear operator $L(\mathbf{k}) = \nu k^2 + \mu$ and precompute exponentials
$$
E_{1/2} = e^{-L\Delta t/2},\qquad E_1 = e^{-L\Delta t}.
$$
Then perform:

1. First nonlinear evaluation: $N_1 = N(\omega) + f$.  
2. Midpoint state: $\omega^\star = E_{1/2}\!\left(\omega + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}N_1\right)$.  
3. Second nonlinear evaluation: $N_2 = N(\omega^\star) + f$ (forcing re-evaluated if time-dependent or stochastic).  
4. Final update: $\displaystyle \omega^{n+1} = E_1\,\omega + \Delta t\,E_{1/2}\,N_2$.

This scheme is second-order accurate and integrates the stiff linear part exactly.

The program returns the physical fields
$$
(\,\omega(x,y),\; u(x,y),\; v(x,y)\,),
$$
which represent the real-space vorticity and velocity associated with the solver's current spectral state.

## Initial Condition

The routine constructs an initial vorticity field $\omega(x,y,t=0)$ whose Fourier coefficients are random but restricted to a narrow annulus in wavenumber space. This produces a smooth, band-limited random field commonly used as an initial condition in 2D turbulence simulations. If the user does not specify the central wavenumber $k_0$, it is taken from the forcing band
$$
k_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}(k_{\min}+k_{\max}),\qquad \text{width} = \tfrac{1}{2}(k_{\max}-k_{\min}).
$$
Otherwise the user-supplied $k_0$ is used and the width defaults to $1$.

The active spectral region is the annulus
$$
\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{k} : |\,|\mathbf{k}| - k_0| \le \text{width}\}.
$$
Define the mask
$$
\chi_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{k}) =
\begin{cases}
1, & |k-k_0|\le\text{width},\\[4pt]
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$
If no grid points satisfy this condition the routine terminates with an error. The code generates complex Gaussian noise
$$
\eta(\mathbf{k}) = \eta_1(\mathbf{k}) + i\,\eta_2(\mathbf{k}),\qquad \eta_1,\eta_2\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1),
$$
and applies the spectral mask $\hat{\omega}(\mathbf{k}) = A\,\eta(\mathbf{k})\,\chi_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{k})$. To obtain a real physical field, the coefficients must satisfy $\hat{\omega}(-\mathbf{k}) = \overline{\hat{\omega}(\mathbf{k})}$. The routine enforces this via the symmetrized assignment
$$
\hat{\omega}(\mathbf{k}) \leftarrow \tfrac{1}{2}\Big(\hat{\omega}(\mathbf{k}) + \overline{\hat{\omega}(-\mathbf{k})}\Big),
$$
and sets the zero mode to zero, $\hat{\omega}(0,0)=0$, so that the initial circulation vanishes.

The current RMS velocity is
$$
U_{\mathrm{rms}}^{\mathrm{current}} = \sqrt{\langle u^2 + v^2\rangle}.
$$
If the user specifies a target RMS velocity $U_{\mathrm{rms}}^{\mathrm{target}}$, the spectrum is rescaled by
$$
\lambda = \frac{U_{\mathrm{rms}}^{\mathrm{target}}}{U_{\mathrm{rms}}^{\mathrm{current}}},\qquad \hat{\omega}_{\mathrm{final}}(\mathbf{k}) = \lambda\,\hat{\omega}(\mathbf{k}).
$$
If the solver defines a de-aliasing mask $\chi_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k})$, the initial condition is projected onto the admissible spectral region
$$
\hat{\omega}_{\mathrm{final}}(\mathbf{k}) \leftarrow \hat{\omega}_{\mathrm{final}}(\mathbf{k})\,\chi_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k}).
$$
The mathematical form of the constructed vorticity field is
$$
\omega_0(x,y) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\Big(\lambda\,A\,\eta(\mathbf{k})\,\chi_{\mathcal{B}}(\mathbf{k})\,\chi_{\mathrm{dealias}}(\mathbf{k})\Big).
$$

## Parameters

The solver advances the two-dimensional vorticity equation
$$
\partial_t\omega + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega = \nu\nabla^2\omega - \mu\omega + f
$$
on a square periodic domain. The simulation parameters used here are
$N=256$, $L=2\pi$, $\nu=10^{-5}$, $\mu=0.02$, and a timestep $\Delta t = 10^{-3}$. A statistically stationary flow is maintained using a random band-limited forcing of amplitude $0.03$ applied at wavenumber $k_f=5$. The total simulated time is $T=1000$, corresponding to $10^6$ timesteps. The initial condition is a band-limited random vorticity field centered at $k_0=2$ and scaled so that the initial RMS velocity satisfies $U_{\mathrm{rms}}=0.1$.

A function `compute_diagnostics` evaluates standard quadratic measures used to quantify two-dimensional turbulence. The kinetic energy is
$$
\mathrm{KE} = \tfrac{1}{2}\langle u^2 + v^2\rangle,
$$
while the enstrophy is
$$
\mathcal{Z} = \tfrac{1}{2}\langle\omega^2\rangle.
$$
Palinstrophy, defined as $P=\langle|\nabla\omega|^2\rangle$, is computed spectrally using $\omega_x = i k_x \omega(\mathbf{k})$ and $\omega_y = i k_y \omega(\mathbf{k})$. It provides the enstrophy-dissipation rate via $\epsilon_\omega = \nu P$. The forcing contributes an enstrophy injection rate $\epsilon = \langle \omega f\rangle$, where $f$ is obtained by inverse transforming the spectral forcing field. These quantities are standard invariants used in the analysis of 2D turbulence.

Several diagnostic Reynolds numbers are computed. The RMS velocity is $U_{\mathrm{rms}} = \sqrt{\langle u^2 + v^2\rangle}$. The large-scale or "box" Reynolds number is
$$
Re_{\mathrm{box}} = \frac{U_{\mathrm{rms}}L}{\nu},
$$
while the forcing-scale Reynolds number is
$$
Re_f = \frac{U_{\mathrm{rms}}}{\nu k_f},
$$
based on the forcing length scale $\ell_f = 1/k_f$. Velocity gradients obtained spectrally yield $\langle|\nabla u|^2\rangle = \langle u_x^2 + u_y^2 + v_x^2 + v_y^2\rangle$, from which the Taylor microscale $\lambda = U_{\mathrm{rms}}/\sqrt{\langle|\nabla u|^2\rangle}$ and Taylor Reynolds number $Re_\lambda = U_{\mathrm{rms}}\lambda/\nu$ are determined. These diagnostic quantities follow standard turbulence theory.

## Time-Loop Outputs

Every 200 steps the solver performs the following tasks: saves the full simulation state $(\omega, u, v, \omega_{\mathbf{k}}, t)$, prints diagnostic quantities and Reynolds numbers, and monitors the evolution of enstrophy, energy, palinstrophy, and the enstrophy injection rate. This produces a complete time-evolution dataset suitable for spectral analysis, cascade studies, and steady-state detection.

The solver integrates the vorticity equation
$$
\partial_t \omega + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\omega = \nu\Delta\omega - \mu\omega + f.
$$

The numerical scheme employs spectral discretization, integrating-factor RK2 time stepping, random band-limited forcing, and dealiasing using the $2/3$ rule. At regular intervals the solver computes
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{KE} &= \tfrac12\langle u^2 + v^2\rangle, & \mathcal{Z} &= \tfrac12\langle \omega^2\rangle,\\
P &= \langle |\nabla\omega|^2\rangle, & \epsilon &= \langle \omega f\rangle,\\
Re_{\mathrm{box}} &= \dfrac{U_{\mathrm{rms}} L}{\nu}, & Re_f &= \dfrac{U_{\mathrm{rms}}}{\nu k_f},\\
Re_\lambda &= \dfrac{U_{\mathrm{rms}}^2}{\nu\sqrt{\langle |\nabla u|^2\rangle}}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The full flow state is written to file for later analysis.
